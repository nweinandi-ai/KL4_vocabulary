What is migration?

Migration means moving from one place to another to live or work there permanently or for a long time. People who move to a different country are called migrants. There are different reasons why people migrate.

Push and pull factors

Push factors are reasons that make people want to leave their home country — for example, poverty, war, persecution, or a lack of jobs.

Pull factors are reasons that attract people to a new country — for example, better job opportunities, political stability, or a higher standard of living.

Types of migrants

A refugee is a person who has been forced to leave their home country because of war or persecution. An economic migrant moves mainly to find work or better living conditions. An asylum seeker has applied for protection in a new country but is waiting for a decision.

Challenges and opportunities

Integration means becoming part of a new society — learning the language, finding work, and making connections. Host countries provide humanitarian aid such as shelter and food. Migrants often face language barriers, discrimination, and difficulties with border control procedures.

Climate change

The Earth's temperature is rising because humans burn fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and gas. This releases greenhouse gases like CO₂ into the atmosphere. The result is global warming, which causes more extreme weather events — droughts, floods, and storms.

Pollution

Pollution means making the environment dirty and harmful. Air pollution is caused by car emissions and factory smoke. Water pollution comes from chemicals and plastic waste entering rivers and oceans. Pollution harms both wildlife and human health.

Loss of biodiversity

Biodiversity refers to the variety of all living things on Earth. When species die out forever, we call this extinction. The main causes are habitat destruction, pollution, and climate change. Protecting biodiversity is crucial for a healthy planet.

What can we do?

Switching to renewable energy (solar, wind, water) reduces emissions. Living more sustainably means consuming less and wasting less. Governments can pass laws to protect habitats and reduce deforestation — the cutting down of large forest areas.

What is globalisation?

Globalisation describes the process by which the world becomes more connected. Countries trade with each other, share ideas, and influence each other's cultures. Thanks to the internet and modern transport, goods, money, and information move around the world faster than ever before.

Advantages and disadvantages

Globalisation creates new jobs and lowers prices for consumers. It allows people to share ideas and technology. However, it can also lead to inequality — rich countries often benefit more than poor ones. Local industries may suffer when they cannot compete with cheap imported goods.

Key players and concepts

Multinational corporations (MNCs) are companies that operate in many countries. They create jobs but are often criticised for paying low wages. Free trade means countries can trade without paying taxes on imported goods (tariffs). International organisations like the WTO help regulate world trade.

Culture and society

Globalisation also affects culture. Cultural exchange means people share music, food, fashion, and ideas across borders. Some people worry that local traditions are lost when global brands dominate. Countries become more interdependent — they rely on each other for trade, energy, and food.
